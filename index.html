Song Popularity Predictor

  • View on Google Colab

For my Foundations of Data Science course (DS 3000), my group and I created a report in Jupyter Notebook detailing our findings in regards to predicting the expected popularity of a piece of music. We used a Spotify Dataset from Kaggle that included approximately 160,000 songs, as well as a scraped dataset from a listing of the top 100 most streamed songs on Spotify. Using various Python libraries and toolkits, we cleaned and visualized the data, and fit it to both regression and classification models. After optimizing our models, we found that on our test sets, the best regressor (K-nearest Neighbors) had an R2 of .3088, and the best classifier (LinearSVC) had an accuracy of 78.43%.

In the report, my contributions involved all of the written portions (excluding the executive summary), as well as all of the code relating to the classification models. I also created two of the visualizations, and also improved upon / added to the other three in addition to their respective analyses. As an example, you can see one of my visualizations below:

A 3D scatter plot can be used to see how Spotify's popularity metric behaves with 2 values: the year released, and the amount of streams. A color scale reflects the rank, with higher ranking songs having lighter shades, and lower ranking songs having darker shades. From this visualization, it can be seen that the popularity value is a very time-based variable, as it has a strong correlation to the release year of the song. Thus, by Spotify's metric, a song can have a high rank with many total streams, but if the streams are not recent, then the popularity score will not be as similarly high.

EasyAnimator

In my Object-Oriented Design class (CS 3500), I pair-programmed a GUI application with my friend Alex Les. Our program is able to read text-based input files (many of which were given to us) to build an animation of simple shapes, including rectangles, ellipses, and plus shapes.

Animations can be displayed visually or textually outputted through tables of animation frame data. In our interactive visual animations, standard playback and speed controls are supported, along with toggles for looping, discrete frame playing, as well as an outline mode for shapes. Users can also insert slow motion commands in the input files to create intervals of slow motion in the animation. Lastly, our application can also export the animations in SVG code to be played in a browser.

Throughout our project, we maintained a robust Model-View-Controller (MVC) design, documented our code throughout, and thoroughly tested each component of the application. In a later part of our assignment, we created fully functional adapters to accomodate for additional view components received from other students' projects. This involved little to no changes in our original code: a direct result of our strong program design (minimal coupling, interface segregation, single class responsibility, etc).

Space Invaders

I developed a simple game of Space Invaders for my Fundamentals of Computer Science II class (CS 2500). The player can use the arrow keys to move side to side and the spacebar for firing from the spaceship.

At most, the invaders can have 10 shots fired on screen at any moment, and the player is also limited at a maximum of 3 shots on screen. Once all the invaders are hit, the player has won. However, if the player gets hit by an Invader bullet, the game ends in a loss.
